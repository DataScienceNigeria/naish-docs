The **Nigeria AI Scaling Hub (NAISH)** is a national AI infrastructure initiative
to deploy and operate one high-performance GPU compute node, providing shared GPU
access under a governed, multi-tenant model. It is at once a piece of physical
infrastructure – a single very capable server – and a programme: a coordinated
effort to take promising Nigerian AI solutions from pilot to national scale on
compute that lives in-country.

## The public initiative

NAISH was launched publicly in **June 2026** by the **Federal Ministry of
Communications, Innovation & Digital Economy (FMCIDE)** together with the **Gates
Foundation**. It is framed as a **multi-stakeholder initiative** that convenes
government agencies, private-sector technology companies, academia, and
development partners to support the large-scale implementation of mature AI.

- **Gates Foundation backing:** providing
  technical assistance, computing infrastructure, policy support, and strategic
  partnerships – explicitly to help move AI innovations **beyond the pilot stage
  to nationwide deployment**.
- **Priority sectors:** health, agriculture, and education.
- **Ambition:** position Nigeria as an exemplar for the responsible, equitable
  adoption of emerging technologies across Africa.

**Lagos Business School (LBS)**, Pan-Atlantic University, is the **primary
recipient** of the Gates Foundation grant: it manages the Hub and runs its
activities, under the leadership of **Prof. Yinka David-West**. **Data Science
Nigeria (DSN)** is the **technical implementation partner**, responsible for
infrastructure design and validation and for the advisory record behind this
documentation.

## Why it exists: the compute-access gap

Access to high-performance compute is the single most consistent bottleneck for
AI development in Africa. The hardware needed to train and run serious models is
expensive, concentrated in a handful of markets, and largely out of reach for
Nigerian researchers, startups, and universities working on locally-relevant
problems. With no national GPU cluster, Nigerian teams have had to depend on
foreign cloud services billed in dollars – a competitive handicap that worsens
with every Naira devaluation, and one that also pushes sensitive national data
off-shore.

NAISH exists to close that gap with **sovereign, shared compute**: a national
resource that lowers the cost barrier, keeps data in-country, and lets teams
focus on building rather than on procuring scarce GPUs.

## Mission

NAISH provides shared compute access supporting a portfolio of **seven identified
national use cases** plus broader access for AI startups and researchers. The
single node is operated as a governed, multi-tenant resource rather than
dedicated hardware for any one team – allocation follows a workload-governance
framework of quotas, priority tiers, and a scheduling calendar rather than
first-come-first-served billing.

## The SAID Challenge

Alongside the Hub, the programme runs the **Scaling AI for Development (SAID)
Challenge** – a scheme that links validated government
challenges with proven AI innovators across Health, Agriculture, Education, Financial Services
and Public Services through structured validation, matching, pilot deployment and impact
monitoring. It is the demand-side complement to the Hub's supply of compute:
SAID surfaces real public-sector problems and startups with proven solutions,
and the Hub provides the infrastructure and support to scale them.


## Hosting

Physical hosting at the **Galaxy Backbone (GBB) Data
Centre in Abuja**. Hosting the node in a national data centre is a deliberate
sovereignty choice: proprietary Nigerian data, including sensitive health and
financial records handled by the use cases, stays on national infrastructure
rather than leaving the country.
